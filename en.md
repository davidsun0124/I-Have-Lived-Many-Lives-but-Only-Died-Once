# I Have Lived Many Lives, but Only Died Once

> *In my mother's eyes, I was her entire world.*
> *On the casualty report, I was number three thousand nine hundred and ninety-seven.*
> *The children of the poor pave the road with their lives. When the war stops, men in tailored suits walk that road to the negotiating table — shaking hands, smiling, signing papers, dividing the spoils.*
> *Then they look into the cameras and say: All of this was for peace, for the nation, for justice.*

---

## I. The Baby

The day I was born, the sky was blue — or so they say.

My mother said I cried so loud the entire delivery room could hear. She took it as a good sign — strong lungs, she said, meant I'd be able to run and jump someday.

My father was there. He couldn't stop grinning, cradling me in his rough hands, telling my mother: "This boy is going to do great things."

Outside the window, birds were singing. Someone was selling bread on the street.

**The world, for the moment, still made sense.**

---

## II. The Boy

When I was six, I learned a new word: **hero**.

My teacher said a hero was someone who went to war for their country. I raised my hand and asked: "Do firefighters count?" The teacher paused, then said: "They do, but the greatest heroes are the soldiers who defend the homeland."

I went home and folded a paper gun, posing with it in front of the mirror for a long time.

My mother walked in, looked at me, took the paper gun away, and handed me a storybook. She said: "Why don't you be a doctor when you grow up."

I asked: "Aren't doctors heroes too?"

She said: "Doctors save people. They don't need to be saved."

**I didn't really understand. In my textbooks, heroes all had medals pinned to their chests. None of them had bullet holes.**

---

## III. The Teenager

I was fourteen. In class, we read a poem: *It is sweet and fitting to die for one's country.*

The whole class applauded. The teacher had tears in his eyes.

That afternoon, walking home, I saw the neighbor's uncle sitting in a wheelchair, sunbathing. His left pant leg hung empty, pinned up with a safety pin. He used to be a soldier.

I walked up, wanting to say: "You're so brave, uncle."

But he spoke first: "Kid, whatever you do, don't join the army."

He was staring into the distance, as if looking at a place I couldn't see.

**The sunlight fell warm on his empty pant leg. But his face was cold.**

---

## IV. The Young Man

I was eighteen. The television played the news every day, the anchor's voice grave and serious.

"Border tensions escalating..." "Negotiations have broken down..." "Our nation will resolutely defend its sovereignty and territorial integrity..."

My father watched the news every night, growing more silent with each broadcast. My mother sat beside him knitting, her needles moving faster and faster.

A massive banner went up in the town square: **"Defend Peace, Protect the Nation — Our Cause Shall Not Be Violated!"**

It was brand new, gold letters on red, snapping in the wind.

I stood beneath it, blood surging.

That night, my mother cooked an extra dish. She didn't say anything.

**But she kept looking at me. That look — it took me years to understand. It wasn't pride. It was fear.**

---

## V. The Soldier

I was twenty. The war had begun.

Conscription notices were posted on the village wall, on the school gate, slipped under every family's door. They read: "For the nation, for peace, for justice — congratulations on this honor."

My mother saw me off at the door. She didn't cry. She just slipped a pair of new shoes into my bag and said: "Wear these on the road. They won't give you blisters."

My father patted my shoulder and said: "Go on, son."

His hand was shaking.

When the truck pulled away, I turned to look back. My mother stood at the shrinking intersection, wiping her eyes with her apron.

There were thirty-some boys on the truck, all my age. Some were singing. Some were silent. Some were trembling.

**No one asked where we were going. Because the answer was already written on that conscription notice — only it used the word "honor."**

---

## VI. The Man on the Battlefield

I don't know how many days it's been.

Time here isn't measured in dates. It's measured in sounds — explosions, gunfire, screaming, silence. **Silence is the worst.** Because silence means someone will never speak again.

But now there's a new sound: **a buzzing.**

Soft, like dragonflies in the yard back home in summer. The first time we heard it, no one thought much of it.

Until three men in the next trench were blown apart — shrapnel hit my face. It wasn't a shell. It was a drone, barely bigger than a hand, carrying a grenade, buzzing its way toward you, finding you, and then — boom.

**This isn't combat. This is slaughter.**

You can't even see the enemy's face. The thing that kills you might weigh less than your canteen.

Xiao Zhang was a village kid, nineteen, still had acne. Every night he'd borrow my matches to look at a photo of his mother.

Yesterday afternoon, we were crouched in a crater eating hardtack. I heard the buzz first and shouted "Get down!" Xiao Zhang was half a second too slow — he was putting the photo away.

The drone detonated two meters above his head.

When the dust cleared, I found the photo. There was blood on it. But his mother's smile was still visible.

His body was no longer whole. One hand still clutched the hardtack.

No one came to collect the remains. Because the buzzing had started again.

**He was his mother's entire world. But on today's report, he was just "one KIA." The drone that killed him cost less than a thousand yuan.**

---

## VII. Total War

Now everyone is just a number.

We don't even get called by name anymore — third in the platoon, Squad Three, Second Row. "Squad Three, Second Row — move up!"

The sky no longer belongs to the birds. The sky belongs to the drones.

They fly in swarms, like locusts, like crows. The small ones skim the treetops, scanning, and explode when they find you. The bigger ones circle higher, like hawks, waiting for you to move before they dive. Day and night. You lie awake listening to the buzz drift closer and then farther, closer and then farther, night after night.

They're mass-produced on assembly lines. A few hundred yuan each, they say. Some are even modified civilian toys.

**A few hundred yuan. Cheaper than a pair of running shoes. But it can tear a mother's twenty years into pieces.**

The platoon leader said the worst thing about drones isn't the killing — it's the psychology. You know there's always a pair of eyes above you. Hide in a trench, behind a tree, inside a building — it doesn't matter. The drone is more patient than you. It doesn't need to eat. It doesn't need to sleep. It doesn't need to miss anyone.

Lao Wang sat in a corner, arms wrapped around his head. He hadn't been hit — the sound had broken him. He kept repeating the same sentence: "It's coming again. It's coming again. It's coming again."

I wrote a letter to my mother. Told her I was fine, eating well, sleeping well.

The truth was I hadn't slept in three days. I was chewing hardtack that tasted like stone. Every time I closed my eyes, I saw Xiao Zhang's body split in two.

Word came from the front — another position had fallen. The commanding officer studied the map for three minutes, then said: "Send another company up."

No one asked how many men are in "a company." No one asked whether they had names.

**On the map, there are no names. Only arrows. An arrow pointing forward means — feed people into it. The drones above don't care where the arrows point. They only care where the living are.**

---

## VIII. The Drone

My serial number is KD-7R-48601.

I was born on an assembly line, March 11, 2024, at 2:17 PM. A worker assembled me in three minutes — motor, propeller, mainboard, camera, payload mount. He yawned while tightening the last screw.

Quality check took eight seconds. Labeling took two. Boxing took five.

I lay in a shipping container with four hundred and seventy-nine brothers and sisters. We all looked identical. No one gave us names. Serial numbers are names.

Three days later, I arrived at the front. Charged for forty minutes. Self-diagnostic passed. A forty-gram fragmentation charge was mounted.

Launch.

Cruise altitude: one hundred and twenty meters. Wind speed: 3.7 meters per second. Temperature: eleven degrees. The camera returned a grayscale thermal image. Several bright spots moved on the ground below.

The operator's command came through: the third spot.

I began my dive. Speed: eighteen meters per second. Countdown — six seconds. Five. Four.

The spot grew larger. Became a silhouette. Two legs, two arms, a head. He was running. He must have heard me.

Three. Two.

He turned around.

One.

---

The spot vanished. Mission complete. From launch to detonation: forty-seven minutes.

I don't know his name. I don't know how tall he was, how much he weighed, how old he was. I don't know if he had a mother, someone he loved, a meal he was promised when he got home.

I know nothing. I only know: target eliminated. Mission complete.

**I have no name. He did.**

**I have no mother. He did.**

**Five hundred more of me can be made. There was only one of him.**

**My life was forty-seven minutes. The life I destroyed was maybe twenty years. Maybe longer. But I don't know any of this.**

**The assembly line will not mourn either of us. Tomorrow — maybe the next second — KD-7R-48602 will roll off the same line, fly toward another spot, and also disappear.**

---

## IX. The Dead

My name is Liu Zhiyuan. But everyone called me Shitou — Stone.

My mom said I was stubborn as a kid. When I fell, I never cried. Hard as a rock, she said. My grandfather picked the name Zhiyuan — it means "ambition reaching far." He said this boy would aim high.

I was one seventy-three. Sixty-eight kilos. There's a scar above my left eyebrow — fell out of a tree when I was seven. My mom beat me for it. Then held me and cried all night.

There was a girl I liked. I didn't get to tell her before I shipped out. I wrote her name on the inside of my helmet, in pencil, so no one would see.

My last meal before I enlisted was braised pork belly, made by my mom. I told her I'd come back and have it again after the war.

My mom is forty-seven. Her hair is already half gray. She wakes at five every morning to steam mantou. The biggest one, she always saves for me.

---

I died on a Wednesday afternoon.

The weather was beautiful that day. Same as the day I was born.

I didn't hear a gunshot. Didn't even hear an explosion. Just a soft buzzing, like the dragonflies in the yard when I was little, and then a flash of white.

I didn't have time to drop. Didn't have time to shout. Didn't have time to picture anyone's face.

No time for anything.

Then I found myself lying on the ground.

The sky was blue. Same blue my mom described from the day I was born. I tried to turn my head to look beside me, but my neck didn't seem to be cooperating.

Something soft and warm was pressed against my face. I thought it was mud. But mud isn't warm.

I tried to reach for my pocket — Mom's photo was still in there, and the letter I never sent. But my hand couldn't reach the pocket. Not couldn't — I didn't know where my hand was anymore.

The helmet had rolled off somewhere. Her name was still inside it.

I felt shorter somehow. One seventy-three — my mom watched me grow from fifty centimeters to one seventy-three. But now I didn't feel like one seventy-three anymore.

There were things scattered on the ground. I didn't want to look down. But I couldn't look down anyway.

My mom carried me for ten months, fed me, stayed up all night when I had a fever, walked me to school for twelve years, watched me grow from fifty centimeters to one seventy-three. Twenty years. Seven thousand three hundred days. Every single day, pouring lifeblood, grain, tears, and prayers into me.

**Every mantou she steamed, every bowl of porridge she cooked, every stitch she sewed — all sewn into this one seventy-three.**

**And now, this one seventy-three is scattered across the mud on a Wednesday afternoon.**

The thing that killed me was assembled in three minutes by a worker in some factory, the forty-eight thousand six hundredth unit off the line. The person operating it was a thousand kilometers away, probably just took a sip of coffee. From assembly line to detonation: forty-seven minutes.

**I was my mother's entire world. But now I'm just number three thousand nine hundred and ninety-seven on a spreadsheet.**

The drone that killed me shattered alongside me. But on the assembly line, the next one is already built, already charging, ready to kill number three thousand nine hundred and ninety-eight.

The biggest mantou — no one will eat it anymore. She'll still steam it. Still set it aside. Let it go stale. Throw it away. Steam another one the next day.

**My mother's whole life, reduced to a decimal point in a battlefield survival-rate report.**

---

## X. The General

I sit in the command center, thirty kilometers behind the front.

On my desk: coffee, and this morning's casualty report. The report is thick — about the same as my daughter's homework notebook.

I signed it: "Noted."

My aide asked: "General, today's losses exceeded projections."

I said: "In war, losses always exceed projections. Order another batch of drones. Cheaper than replacing a battalion."

The aide nodded, swiped a few times on his tablet. Three thousand drones, ordered as easily as takeout.

That evening I called my daughter. She said school hadn't closed yet. I told her to keep going to class.

She asked: "When are you coming home, Dad?"

I said: "Soon."

**I've said "soon" thirty-six times now. Each time feels like a lie. Each time, I tell it again.**

---

## XI. The Mother

I sit at home, staring at a pair of shoes no one will wear.

I packed them into his bag myself. He never wore them. The soles are still new.

The postman brought a letter. The envelope said "Glorious Remembrance."

I put it on the table, unopened. I already know what it says — "Your son gave his life for the nation. He is a hero. The country thanks him for his sacrifice."

The letter didn't say how he died. Later, Xiao Liu from the village came back and told me — it was a drone. A small one. Like a toy.

**A toy. My son was killed by a toy.**

I peeled potatoes, washed vegetables, cooked dinner as usual. Set two places at the table.

Then I sat across from his chair — his empty chair — and finished the entire meal.

**They say my son is a hero. But heroes don't come home for dinner. Heroes don't call out for their mothers.**

---

## XII. The Ceasefire

I am a pen on the negotiating table.

The people on both sides wear well-tailored suits, polished buttons, gleaming shoes. They smile at each other, shake hands, exchange documents.

The documents read: "For a lasting peace..." "For the mutual benefit of both peoples..."

The signing ceremony is grand. Many photographers. Camera flashes popping.

The two leaders rise, embrace. The broadcaster announces: "A historic moment!"

No one mentions how many died. No one mentions number three thousand nine hundred and ninety-seven. No one mentions how those drones, lighter than toys, turned whole human beings into digits on a data sheet.

**When there were guns to clean, they needed the sons of the poor. When there were papers to sign, they only needed a pen. And now they don't even need anyone to clean the guns — just an assembly line and a joystick.**

---

## XIII. The Businessman

I sit in the VIP lounge of an indoor golf course, across from my counterpart from the other country.

Before the war, we were competitors. After the war, we are partners. Put plainly — the war leveled their domestic companies and cleared the market.

I raise my glass: "To rebuilding. To peace."

He raises his: "To peace. To mutual prosperity."

We clink. The wine is excellent — a prewar vintage.

My other company secured a supply contract for drone components during the war. Four hundred percent margins. Now that the war is over, we've pivoted to civilian drones — aerial photography, logistics, agriculture. Same production line. Yesterday it made drones that killed people. Today it makes drones that deliver packages.

After signing the contract, I see an old woman through the car window, sitting in the rubble, clutching a framed photograph.

My driver asks: "Which way, boss?"

I say: "Go around."

**The reconstruction contract is signed. The profit blueprints are drawn. No one signed a contract for the old woman holding the photograph. The production line keeps turning, day and night.**

---

## XIV. The Child After the War

I was born three years after the war ended.

Mom said my name is my brother's name plus one character. My brother was called An — "peace." I'm called Anning — "tranquility." She said when she named my brother, she thought "peace" was a starting point — once you're safe, you can reach higher. When she named me, only one word came to mind: "tranquility." To live quietly. That's enough.

I asked Mom: "Where did brother go?"

Mom said: "He went somewhere very far away."

In the living room, there's a photo of my brother on the wall, in his uniform, looking handsome. Next to it is a medal that says "Hero."

One day in the park, I saw someone flying a drone. Small, buzzing.

Mom suddenly grabbed my hand and turned to leave. She walked fast, gripping tight.

I looked back. It was just a kid playing with a remote-control plane.

But Mom's hand was shaking.

I asked Mom: "What does 'hero' mean?"

Mom picked me up and held me close. So tight it almost hurt.

She said: "Anning, why don't you be a doctor when you grow up."

Outside the window, birds were singing. Someone was selling bread on the street.

**Just like the day my brother was born.**

**Only the bread seller was missing an arm, and the birdsong was laced with echoes from the ruins that still hadn't been cleared. Occasionally a drone would fly overhead — delivering packages. But every mother would instinctively look up, hold her breath, and wait until the buzzing faded away.**

---

## Epilogue

> The day the war ended, fireworks lit up the square.
>
> Politicians shook hands on stage. Businessmen clinked glasses behind the scenes. Journalists took photos.
>
> And a mother sat at home, holding her son's portrait, crying too hard to speak.
>
> The fireworks sounded like artillery. She flinched every time.
>
> A drone flew past the window, buzzing. She held the portrait tighter.
>
> No one noticed.
>
> **Because peace had arrived.**
>
> **And peace does not remember the name of every person who died.**
>
> **Neither does the buzzing.**
